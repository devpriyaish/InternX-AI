### Eyes and Vision

Humans have **trichromatic vision**, meaning we can perceive the full range of colors through three types of cone cells in the eyes. Most carnivorous animals are **dichromatic**; they cannot see the full color spectrum the way humans can.

Why? Because herbivores need to distinguish fruits, leaves, and plants based on color — for example, whether a fruit is ripe or spoiled. Carnivores, on the other hand, depend more on detecting motion because they hunt moving prey.

This suggests that human vision is more aligned with plant-eating species.

### Saliva and Digestion

Human saliva contains an enzyme called **salivary amylase**, which begins digesting starch right in the mouth. Carnivores do not have this enzyme because meat contains little starch.

This means the human digestive system is naturally designed to process carbohydrate-rich foods like grains and vegetables.

### Stomach and Intestines

Carnivores usually eat large meals infrequently, so they have very large stomachs and short intestines. Meat rots quickly, so it needs to pass through the body rapidly.

Humans are the opposite. Our stomach is relatively small, and our intestines are very long — about eight times the length of the body — similar to herbivorous animals. Plant food rich in fiber takes longer to digest, which is why herbivores need longer intestines.

### Hands, Nails, and Hunting Ability

Carnivores have powerful claws and limbs designed to catch and kill prey. Human nails are weak. We are not physically equipped to hunt animals with our bare hands.

### Sweating vs Panting

Carnivorous animals cool themselves mainly by panting. Humans cool themselves by sweating, which is more common among herbivorous species.

So from teeth, saliva, vision, digestion, skin, and body structure — everything indicates that the human body is naturally more suited for plant-based food.
